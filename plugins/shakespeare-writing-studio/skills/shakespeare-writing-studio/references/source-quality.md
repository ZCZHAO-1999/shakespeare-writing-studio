# Source Quality / Background Search Rules

Background research must raise the quality of the article. It must never become random internet filler.

## 1. Hard Rule

When background search is required, use only top-tier, relevant sources. Do not assemble an article from generic SEO pages, unsourced blogs, AI summaries, listicles, content farms, or random statistics.

## 2. Tier 1 Sources

Prefer these whenever possible:

- official government or statistical agencies
- company filings, annual reports, investor relations pages
- peer-reviewed papers or reputable academic institutions
- primary datasets
- official product/platform documentation
- reputable research organizations
- high-quality journalism with named reporting and clear sourcing
- books, speeches, interviews, or essays by recognized domain experts

## 3. Tier 2 Sources

Use only as supporting context:

- respected industry newsletters
- credible analyst notes with transparent methodology
- conference talks from known practitioners
- expert blogs with original analysis
- reputable podcasts/interviews with primary participants

## 4. Avoid

- SEO explainers
- anonymous market-size posts
- auto-generated trend pages
- copied charts without original source
- social media claims without corroboration
- AI-generated summaries
- random Medium posts used as evidence
- low-quality listicles

## 5. Top-Tier Style Sampling

If studying style, only sample writing from strong publications or writers relevant to the target format.

Examples:

- Medium/Substack: top essays from known writers, editors, founders, operators, researchers, or publications.
- Business/technology: Bloomberg, The Information, Stratechery, The Atlantic, Wired, FT, The Economist, a16z, First Round Review, Lenny's Newsletter, Every, Not Boring, or equivalent high-signal sources.
- Academic/research: papers, labs, university publications, or reputable research institutes.

Do not imitate surface style. Extract writing moves: opening, structure, evidence rhythm, sentence texture, and ending.

## 6. Research Log

For every researched piece, create a source log:

| Use | Source | Why It Is High Quality | Date | Link | Kept / Rejected |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|

Rejected sources should be listed when they were considered but not trusted.

## 7. If Good Sources Are Not Available

Do not fill the gap with weak sources.

Use:

- `[TK: source needed]`
- a softened claim
- a narrower claim
- a note in `publishing-note.md`

The article should be less ambitious before it becomes poorly sourced.
